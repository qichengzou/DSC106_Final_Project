The Peak Comes Early

The American West's water shows up months before anyone can use it — and warming is widening that gap. CMIP6 models show the Sierra Nevada's snowmelt arriving earlier in the year and carrying far less water.

↓ scroll to explore
Scene 1 of 6 · The cause · AND
It's not how much falls.
It's what falls — and when it melts
The Sierra's cold season sits close to freezing. That balance point is everything: a few degrees of warming flips more winter precipitation from snow to rain, and melts the snow that does fall earlier and faster. The mountain isn't getting less weather — it's getting less storage.
Near 0°C, small warming = big change
Cold-season temp → rain vs. snow
Spring temp → melt timing

Scene 3 of 6 · The shift · BUT
And it arrives
earlier
Warming changes when the water comes. On a water-year axis, the ensemble-mean melt peak slides from March to February — about a month earlier — and the whole curve shrinks by nearly half. Normalize each curve to isolate timing; switch to absolute units to see the volume collapse.

Peak melt: ~1.19 → ~0.61 mm/day
Apr–Jul share: 16% → 9%

Scene 4 of 6 · The plumbing
This snow isn't abstract —
it's the plumbing
Meltwater from these peaks feeds the Sacramento and San Joaquin rivers, which converge in the Delta and irrigate the Central Valley — one of the most productive farming regions on earth. Change the melt, and every link downstream feels it.
Sierra snow → rivers → Delta
→ Central Valley farms & cities

Scene 5 of 6 · The consequence · THEREFORE
Earlier and smaller —
still out of step with summer
California's farms draw the most water in mid-to-late summer, months after the melt peak. Reservoirs exist to bridge that gap. A pulse that's now earlier and smaller means more of the year's water must be captured and stored, with less margin in dry years. The mismatch doesn't guarantee shortage — but it raises the stress on every buffer in the system.
Melt peak: Feb–Mar  |  Demand peak: ~July
~30% of CA water begins as Sierra snow
Frame as RISK, not certain shortage

Scene 6 of 6 · The choice · THEREFORE
How bad
depends on us
These outcomes aren't fixed. Under moderate emissions (SSP2-4.5) the melt still shifts and shrinks — but far less than under high emissions (SSP5-8.5). The gap between the two paths widens through the century. The mountain's future is, to a real degree, a choice.
Peak melt: 0.75 (SSP2-4.5) vs 0.61 (SSP5-8.5) mm/day
Apr–Jul share: 16% → 15% → 9%
Ends in a choice, not doom

Less Snowmelt, Arriving Sooner

The headline isn't just "less snow." It's less, and sooner — a frozen reservoir built to release water slowly is being pushed to release less of it, earlier, before the dry season needs it. How wide the gap gets is still a choice.
