Step 1 of 4 · Timing
The West's natural water tower
The Sierra Nevada acts as a natural water tower for California. In these CMIP6 simulations, regional runoff is strongest in winter, when storms and snowpack both contribute to river flow.
~30% of California's water supply
originates as Sierra snowpack
Step 2 of 4 · The problem
Runoff peaks in winter.
Demand peaks in August.
Historically, the ensemble mean runoff peaks around February, then falls through spring and summer. Illustrative agricultural demand still peaks in July–August. Reservoirs store winter flows for the irrigation season.
Step 3 of 4 · The change
Warming lowers runoff —
especially in spring
Under SSP5-8.5, the winter peak stays in the same season but drops in magnitude. Runoff from March through June is much lower than in the historical period — less water moving through the basin when storage and soils would normally recharge.
Feb peak: ~100 → ~80 (indexed)
Jun runoff: ~10 → ~1 (indexed)
Peak month: unchanged in this ensemble
Step 4 of 4 · Consequence
Less winter water,
same summer demand
The stress is twofold: less total runoff under high emissions, and the same seasonal split — wet winter flows versus peak irrigation need months later. Systems that rely on storing winter surplus have less to draw on in dry years.

Less Runoff + Seasonal Mismatch

These CMIP6 projections point to reduced Sierra runoff under SSP5-8.5 — not a large shift in peak month in this ensemble, but less water when the wet season arrives and even less heading into summer demand.

Two Futures.
One Choice.

The outcome isn't fixed. Under moderate emissions (SSP2-4.5) the West faces stress but retains meaningful spring runoff. Under high emissions (SSP5-8.5) the loss is sharper. The difference is the emissions path we choose.
