Scene 3 of 4 · Timing
The West's natural water tower
Every winter, the Sierra Nevada stores billions of gallons of water as snow. Each spring, that snow melts slowly — acting like a giant reservoir with no walls, feeding rivers through the dry summer months.
~30% of California's water supply
originates as Sierra snowpack
Scene 3 of 4 · The problem
Melt peaks in June.
Demand peaks in August.
Historically, this worked. Snowmelt peaked in June, just as agricultural demand was ramping up. The curves overlapped enough that reservoirs could buffer the difference.
Scene 3 of 4 · The shift
Warmer springs pull the peak earlier
Under high-emissions projections, the melt peak shifts from June toward April. The water arrives — and runs off — before farms need it. By the time August demand peaks, the snowpack is gone.
Peak melt: June → April
Water demand peak: unchanged, July–Aug
Gap grows by ~6–8 weeks
Scene 3 of 4 · Consequence
The reservoir empties
before demand arrives
This isn't just less water — it's water arriving at the wrong time. Infrastructure built for a June melt can't easily adapt to April floods followed by August drought. The gap between when water comes and when it's needed is the real crisis.

Volume + Timing = Crisis

Even if total snowfall stayed the same, a shifted melt season would destabilize water systems built around historical patterns.
